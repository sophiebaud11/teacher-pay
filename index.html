How much do teachers make?

A lot of people talk about this question. We decided to look at one aspect of the data—geography—to get a sense of how where you teach affects what you make.

It turns out pay really depends on where you teach.

Average teacher salary varies widely by state. As you can see in this graph, the average teacher in Mississippi makes nearly $40,000 less than the average teacher in New York.

Things get even more complex on the county level.

Not only does the number of teachers differ signficantly between counties, but the average weekly wage for teachers does too. In this graph, you can see that the areas with higher weekly wages and higher teacher populations tend to be clustered around big cities like Boston or Chicago.

And even within counties, pay differs widely by school district.

We can see this in action by taking a closer look at the school districts of three states. New York has far more districts than Florida or Nebraska, but the teacher salary within each district varies widely. So, although on average teachers in New York make more than those in other states, this is complicated by the sheer number of districts in the state.

How do these salaries compare to national numbers?

Despite the fact that more than half of public school teachers in the U.S. hold a postbaccalaureate degree, teachers make less than Bachelor's degree holders on average.

But even these numbers can't tell us the full story.

How far someone’s salary can stretch is impacted by other factors in their life, beyond location and occupation. Do they have kids? Do they have a partner? Do they prioritize saving? How much money do they spend on items other than food or shelter?

So, how much do teachers make?

The average teacher pay in the United States, as of 2018, is: $61,730.

But, as we've learned, that number varies widely by state, county, and even district. All of this is complicated even more by the different conditions of each teacher's life.

There’s also a lot this salary doesn’t tell us: like how much the average teacher spends out of pocket for school supplies each year, or how many extra jobs they work to make ends meet.

Which means our answer is: it depends a lot on where you teach.

But should it, especially when many teachers don't make enough to satisfy their cost of living?
